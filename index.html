Written By Ramnick Francis Ramos

On December 2019, a worldwide pandemic caused by a novel virus started in Wuhan, China: the Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2 which causes COVID-19. Since then, we often hear the term “flatten the curve”. But what does that really mean? Why do we need to do it? First, we ought to know what compartmental modelling is.

Compartmental modelling in epidemiology classifies the population into compartments.On the onset of COVID-19 Pandemic, the World Health Organization used an SEIR Model to forecast the outbreak.SEIR stands for the compartments it uses:

Susceptible, those who are able to become infected;

Exposed, those who had been infected but can’t spread the virus;

Infectious, those who are able to spread the virus; and

Removed, those who either had survived or died because of COVID-19.

Imagine that we have a set of marble, and we let them perpetually roll in closed region – causing them to bump with each other. This represents us in an open economy.

But what happens a COVID-19 Positive marble is introduced?

When an infectious individual enters the population, the infectious compartment will exponentially grow as it affects the susceptible compartment. COVID-19 positive individuals that are asymptomatic are categorized to the Exposed Compartment. With the assumption that asymptomatic individuals have low chances of being infectious, Exposed compartment cannot infect other people. And after some time, these infected individuals will either survive or die, they will then be a part of the removed compartment.

You will observe that the number of individuals per category through time is graphed by a curve. This is the curve that we are trying to flatten.

But why “flatten the curve”? If the spike of COVID-19’s Infectious curve exceed the capacity of our health facilities, more people will die.

To fully gauge what this mean, we must know that these curves are governed with their corresponding Ordinary Differential Equations. Simply put, these equations dictate the rate of change of the number of individuals per compartment, which is directly proportional to the incubation and infectious period of the disease and to the contact rate of the population.

For us ordinary individuals, what we can do is follow certain measures mandated by the World Health Organization: Social Distancing and Quarantining of Infected Individuals. You will be surprised on how this will drastically “flatten the curve.”

With the tens of thousands of people dying worldwide because of COVID-19 | and a vaccine that is still yet-to-be-developed, flattening the curve is perhaps the most imperative thing we should focus on. But you, will you take your part on flattening the curve?
